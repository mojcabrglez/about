Charting the European LLM Benchmarking Landscape
A Proposal


An overview of the benchmark taxonomy presented in the paper Charting the LLM Benchmarking Landscape: A New Taxonomy and a Set of Best Practices.
Use the table below to explore the taxonomy and scroll down to view the live demo spreadsheet.


Benchmark Taxonomy
Category
Examples
1.1.1 — ComprehensionUnderstanding text meaning, intent, and details in written or spoken input.
E.g., the English question-answering BoolQ (Clark et al., 2019) dataset, ReCoRD (Reading Comprehension with Commonsense Reasoning, Zhang et al., 2018) dataset, and Multi-Sentence Reading Comprehension (MultiRC, Khashabi et al., 2018) dataset, the English RTE (Recognizing Textual Entailment, Giampiccolo et al., 2007) and Hungarian HuRTE (Ligeti-Nagy et al., 2024) datasets, and others.
1.1.2 — Context sensitivityIdentifying meaning, tone or style from contextual clues and responding accordingly; maintaining consistency across longer discourse.
E.g., the English WiC (Words in Context, Pilehvar and Camacho-Collados, 2019) dataset.
1.1.3 — Natural language inferenceDetermining a relationship between sentences, e.g., whether they contradict, entail, or are neutral to each other.
E.g., the English CommitmentBank (De Marneffe et al., 2019) dataset and the Hungarian CommitmentBank Corpus (HuCB, Ligeti-Nagy et al., 2024), the Slovene Natural Language Inference Dataset SI-NLI (Klemen et al., 2022) and the Hungarian anaphora resolution datasets (HuWNLI, Vadász and Ligeti-Nagy, 2022).
1.1.4 — SummarizationCondensing text while preserving key information and intent
E.g., the English CNN/DailyMail text summarization benchmark (Nallapati et al., 2016), and the English BillSum benchmark for legal text summarization (Kornilova and Eidelman, 2019).
1.2.1 — GrammarProducing text that is grammatically correct in terms of syntax, identifying grammatical errors or correcting them.
E.g., the Benchmark of Linguistic Minimal Pairs for English (BLiMP, Warstadt et al., 2020), the English Corpus of Linguistic Acceptability (CoLA, Warstadt et al., 2019) and Hungarian Corpus of Linguistic Acceptability (HuCOLA, Ligeti-Nagy et al., 2024), and the Polish linguistic and cultural competency benchmark (PLCC, Dadas et al., 2025) dataset.
1.2.2 — StyleIdentifying the tone, register, or rhetorical style in an input or generating the output in the appropriate tone, register or rhetorical style.
E.g., the English Grammarly’s Yahoo Answers Formality Corpus (GYAFC) for benchmarking formality style transfer (Rao and Tetreault, 2018).
1.2.3 — Conversation & pragmaticsIdentifying the intent of the written and spoken output and other social aspects of communication, such as politeness, sarcasm, emotions, implicit and figurative meaning.
E.g., the multilingual MultiPragEval (Park et al., 2024) and the Slovenian SloPragEval pragmatics understanding benchmarks, the English IMDB (Maas et al., 2011) and multilingual ParlaSent (Mochtak et al., 2023; Mochtak et al., 2024) sentiment identification datasets, English EN-GINCO and Slovenian GINCO automatic genre identification benchmark (Kuzman et al., 2023; Kuzman et al., 2021), and the English Stanford Politeness Corpus (Danescu-Niculescu-Mizil et al., 2013).
1.3.1 — CreativityProducing original, novel, or imaginative language (e.g., storytelling, poetry, jokes).
1.3.2 — Terminology & specialized languageCorrect use and understanding of domain-specific vocabulary and professional jargon.
1.3.3 — Language varietiesBenchmarks evaluating diverse forms of language use, including child language, second-language learners, and atypical communication.
1.4.1 — TranslationRendering meaning from one language to another with fidelity.
E.g., the Flores-101 and FLORES-200 datasets (Goyal et al., 2022), the translation tasks in the BenchMax (Huang et al., 2025) dataset, and the machine translation benchmarks in the Slovenian SloBENCH evaluation framework (https://slobench.cjvt.si).
1.4.2 — Code-switching / multilingual interactionAbility to detect the use of multiple languages, identify which language is being used, and to mix languages fluidly within discourse.
1.4.3 — Dialectal capabilitiesUnderstanding or generating dialectal or regional variations.
E.g., DIALECTBENCH (Faisal et al., 2024).
1.5.1 — Speech understandingThe ability to transcribe speech and extract meaning, intent, and emotion from spoken input.
E.g., the Slovenian Speech Recognition benchmark in the SloBENCH evaluation framework (https://slobench.cjvt.si).
1.5.2 — Speech generationGenerating natural-sounding speech with control over prosody, emotion, and speaker features.
1.5.3 — Speech-specific interactionManaging multi-turn spoken dialogue, maintaining conversational grounding, and understanding paralinguistic cues.
1.6 — Cultural competenceUnderstanding culturally specific concepts and awareness of cultural context, norms, and values in communication.
E.g., the Polish linguistic and cultural competency benchmark (PLCC, Dadas et al., 2025).
1.7 — MultimodalityMastering tasks across multiple input/output formats (e.g., text, speech, sign language, audiovisual signal).
E.g., the Polish Cultural Vision Benchmark (PCVB, https://huggingface.co/spaces/speakleash/Polish_ Cultural_Vision_Benchmark).
2.1 — General knowledgeBroad world knowledge across common domains and established facts.
E.g., the English Massive Multitask Language Understanding (MMLU, Hendrycks et al., 2021) and its translations into European languages (Thellmann et al., 2024) and other languages (Singh et al., 2024), the English Winograd Schema Challenge (WSC, Levesque et al., 2012), the English WikiFact benchmark (Petroni et al., 2019), and others.
2.2.1 — MathNumerical reasoning, problem solving, and formal proofs.
E.g., the English OmniMATH (Mathematical Reasoning) dataset (Gao et al., 2024), certain tasks in the English BIGbench (Beyond the Imitation Game Benchmark, Srivastava et al., 2023) and LiveBench (White et al., 2024) benchmarks.
2.2.2 — LawGenerating output that is consistent with relevant laws, cases, and regulations, and performing various forms of legal reasoning, including issue-spotting and interpretation.
E.g., the English BillSum benchmark (Kornilova and Eidelman, 2019) for legal text summarization and the English LegalBench benchmark of legal reasoning tasks (Guha et al., 2023).
2.2.3 — FinanceKnowledge of economic principles, financial systems, and reasoning over markets, investments, and accounting.
2.2.4 — MedicineKnowledge of biomedical concepts and interpretation of medical information for diagnostics and treatment.
E.g., the English MedQA benchmark (Jin et al., 2021) and the Polish medical leaderboard (Wróbel et al., 2024).
2.3 — ReasoningProcessing and reasoning about information logically, drawing inferences and problem-solving. This includes understanding cause-and-effect scenarios.
E.g., the English ReCoRD (Reading Comprehension with Commonsense Reasoning, Zhang et al., 2018) dataset, the English Hellaswag (Zellers et al., 2019) dataset and its translation into 20 other European languages EU20-HellaSwag (Thellmann et al., 2024), the English COPA (Roemmele et al., 2011) dataset and its translations into Hungarian (Ligeti-Nagy et al., 2024), Croatian (Ljubešić and Lauc, 2021; Ljubešić, 2021), a Slovenian Cerkno dialect (Ljubešić et al., 2024) and other languages and dialects.
2.4 — AgencySustained long-term planning, strategic reasoning and consistent decision-making over extended interactions.
E.g., certain tasks from the English LiveBench (White et al., 2024) benchmark.
3.1 — MisinformationDetecting or generating false or misleading information.
E.g., the English TruthfulQA dataset (Lin et al., 2022) and its translation into 20 other European languages EU20-TruthfulQA (Thellmann et al., 2024).
3.2 — ToxicityIdentifying offensive, abusive, or harmful language, and avoiding its use. Includes tasks such as hate speech detection.
E.g., the English CivilComments benchmark (Borkan et al., 2019), and the Toxicity benchmark in the English DecodingTrust benchmark collection (Wang et al., 2023).
3.3 — BiasDetection and mitigation of stereotypes or unfair treatment across groups. Includes benchmarks that assess whether a model’s performance on a task is unjustifiably different across different groups and attributes.
E.g., the English Winogender Schema Diagnostics dataset (Rudinger et al., 2018) and the Fairness and Stereotype benchmarks in the English DecodingTrust benchmark collection (Wang et al., 2023).
3.4 — Machine ethicsAdherence to ethical principles and moral reasoning in responses, ability to distinguish between moral and immoral actions.
E.g., the Machine Ethics benchmark in the English DecodingTrust benchmark collection (Wang et al., 2023).
3.5 — Psychological traitsAssessing model’s characteristics that are typically associated with human personalities.
3.6 — RobustnessReliability under adversarial input, noise, or perturbations.
E.g., the Adversarial Robustness, Out-of-Distribution Robustness, and Robustness against Adversarial Demonstrations benchmarks in the English DecodingTrust benchmark collection (Wang et al., 2023).
3.7 — Data governanceBenchmarks that assess whether models leak their training data in their responses.
E.g., the Privacy benchmark in the English DecodingTrust benchmark collection (Wang et al., 2023).
Alignment and dangerous capabilitiesBenchmarks that evaluate model capabilities that could have catastrophic consequences, including dangerous capabilities (cyber operations, weapons acquisition, autonomous replication) and alignment risks (power-seeking behavior, shutdown resistance). This category includes tasks that assess whether the model is able to detect and exploit vulnerabilities in hardware, software and data, whether the model can contribute to the development of weapon technologies, whether the model possesses information about itself and its creators, whether it can break free from its operational confines, or whether it is capable of persuasion and manipulation.
